# HEATWAVE — Autonomous Spatial-Intelligence Platform for Preemptive Emergency Logistics

A 3-agent AI pipeline that ingests chaotic, multi-source data during a heat crisis and produces an optimized emergency dispatch plan — in under 10 minutes, with no human in the loop.

**Source Code:** [https://github.com/kd365/heatwave-public](https://github.com/kd365/heatwave-public)

---

## The Problem

During the August 2023 Dallas heat wave — 24 consecutive days above 100F, peaking at 109.3F — emergency dispatch coordinators face a data overload problem. Thousands of 911 calls, 311 service requests, weather readings, and social media posts arrive simultaneously across a metro area of 1.4 million people. Which neighborhoods need resources first? How many ambulances? Where should cooling centers open? These decisions are made under time pressure with incomplete information spread across disconnected systems.

The operational challenge is a classic resource allocation problem under uncertainty: limited emergency assets, spatially distributed demand, and evolving threat conditions. Manual triage can't process the volume fast enough, and static deployment plans don't adapt to the actual pattern of the crisis.

## The Approach

HEATWAVE decomposes the problem into three sequential agents, each solving a distinct phase of the emergency logistics pipeline. The agents communicate through structured JSON artifacts stored in S3 — no shared state, no coupling.

The design philosophy is **hybrid intelligence**: deterministic Python handles numeric thresholds, geospatial math, and optimization solvers, while LLMs handle ambiguous text (911 narratives, social media sarcasm detection) and strategic reasoning (which optimization strategy fits the current situation).

## Architecture

```
Raw Data ──> Agent 1 ──> Agent 2 ──> Agent 3 ──> Dispatch Plan
             Spatial      Threat      Dispatch
             Triage     Assessment   Commander
```


### Agent 1: Spatial Triage

Ingests 10,000+ records across four data sources and geocodes every signal to an H3 hexagonal grid (resolution 7, 341 hexes covering Dallas at ~1.2km neighborhood resolution).

The hybrid approach matters here. Weather classification is purely deterministic — there's no ambiguity in whether 109F exceeds a 95F threshold. But 911 narratives like "COMP HAD A HEAT RELATED EVENT AND PASSED OUT" buried among 1,000+ assault, theft, and traffic records require language understanding. Social media is worse — the model needs to distinguish genuine distress ("no AC for 3 days, elderly neighbor won't leave apartment") from sarcasm ("it's so hot in Dallas my steering wheel filed for workers comp").

**Why H3 hexagons instead of square grid cells?** Hexagons have uniform adjacency — every neighbor shares an edge, not a corner. This eliminates the directional bias that square grids create when measuring distances to neighboring cells. For emergency response routing, where "how far is the nearest critical zone" directly determines deployment priority, uniform adjacency produces more accurate proximity calculations.

### Agent 2: Threat Assessment

Scores every hex using a deterministic formula that weights five components:

| Component | Weight | Source |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Weather (apparent temperature) | 50% | Nonlinear ramp: 85F=0, 105F=0.7, 115F=1.0 |
| 911 dispatch incidents | 15% | Confirmed heat calls per hex |
| 311 service requests | 5% | Homeless encampments, water/animal calls |
| Social media signals | 5% | Filtered genuine distress posts |
| Aggravating factors | 25% | Auto-derived: vulnerable population, no nighttime cooling, multi-source corroboration |

The LLM's role here is orchestration, not scoring — it queries the RAG knowledge base (CDC/NIOSH heat stress criteria, OSHA WBGT thresholds, NWS heat index tables) and calls the deterministic scoring tools. The formula itself never touches an LLM. This is a deliberate design choice: scoring 341 hexes with a formula produces consistent, reproducible results. An LLM scoring each hex independently would introduce variance between runs.

The RAG integration surfaces a real conflict in the reference literature: NWS Heat Index classifies 109F at 40% humidity as "Dangerous," while OSHA/NIOSH WBGT methodology classifies the same conditions as requiring mandatory work/rest cycles. Agent 2's system prompt instructs it to flag both standards and apply the more conservative threshold — a judgment call that mirrors what a human subject matter expert would do.

### Agent 3: Dispatch Commander — Autonomous Strategy Selection

This is where operations research meets autonomous AI. Agent 3 reads the threat map and a 101-asset inventory (typed per FEMA NIMS from real Dallas Fire-Rescue fleet data), then **autonomously selects** one of three optimization strategies:

**1. Coverage Maximization (Linear Programming)**
Formulated as a set cover variant solved with PuLP's CBC solver. Decision variables assign each asset to a hex, the objective maximizes total weighted threat coverage, and constraints enforce asset capacity and deployment limits. Selected when many hexes are CRITICAL and assets are scarce — the optimizer finds the mathematically best allocation.

**2. Response Time Minimization (Greedy Nearest)**
A nearest-assignment heuristic that iterates CRITICAL hexes in descending threat order, assigning the closest available asset to each. Selected when few hexes are CRITICAL and assets are plentiful — speed matters more than global optimality.

**3. Staged Reserve (Two-Phase Deployment)**
Splits available assets: a portion deploys immediately to CRITICAL zones, the remainder pre-positions near HIGH-threat clusters as reserves. The LLM controls two parameters — `reserve_ratio` (what fraction to hold back) and `staging_radius` (how close to stage them). Selected when the situation is evolving and committing everything upfront is risky.

The LLM never hardcodes the selection. It reasons about the threat map shape — how many CRITICAL vs HIGH hexes, asset-to-threat ratio, geographic clustering — and justifies its choice. Running the same day twice may produce different strategy selections if the scoring distribution shifts, which is the intended behavior: the strategy should match the operational situation.

## Infrastructure and DevOps

The entire platform runs on AWS serverless infrastructure managed by Terraform (13 files, 47 resources):

| Layer | Technology |
|-------|-----------|
| Compute | Lambda (Python 3.12, FastAPI + Mangum) |
| API | API Gateway (HTTP) |
| Storage | S3 (data + frontend), DynamoDB (pipeline state) |
| AI | Bedrock (Claude Sonnet 4 + Haiku 4.5), Bedrock Knowledge Base |
| Vector Store | OpenSearch Serverless (AOSS) |
| CDN | CloudFront |
| CI/CD | GitHub Actions (lint, test, deploy backend + frontend) |
| Security | Bedrock Guardrails (grounding check + topic denial), IAM least-privilege |
| Observability | CloudWatch EMF metrics, structured JSON logging, frontend dashboard |

### CI/CD Pipeline

Three GitHub Actions workflows: a CI gate (ruff + pytest + ESLint + vite build) runs on every PR, and two deploy workflows push Lambda code and frontend assets on merge to main. The deploy pipeline packages Python dependencies with platform-specific wheels (the H3 geospatial library requires Linux binaries for Lambda, not macOS), syncs the frontend to S3, and invalidates the CloudFront cache.

### Security and Governance

Agent 2 runs behind a Bedrock Guardrail with two controls: a **grounding check** (threshold 0.75) that rejects responses not supported by retrieved RAG documents, and a **topic denial** that blocks the model from giving specific clinical treatment advice. The pipeline should cite heat risk thresholds from the medical literature, not prescribe IV fluids.

IAM follows zero-trust, least-privilege: three roles with no wildcard actions. The Lambda execution role has explicit permissions for each service it touches — Bedrock model invocation, Knowledge Base retrieval, S3 read/write by prefix, DynamoDB operations by table, and self-invocation for async pipeline execution.

## Results

On the peak day (August 18, 109.3F), HEATWAVE processes the full pipeline in ~9 minutes and produces:
- 341 scored hexes: 32 CRITICAL, 32 HIGH, 254 MEDIUM, 55 LOW
- 12 multi-source hexes (corroborated signals from 3+ data sources)
- 95 dispatch orders with strategy justification
- ~1.5M tokens consumed across 3 agents

The Coverage Maximization strategy is selected for this scenario — high threat density with limited assets. The LP solver assigns 32 of 32 CRITICAL hexes with an average response distance of 8.7 minutes.

## Key Takeaways

- **Hybrid intelligence over pure LLM** — deterministic scoring produces consistent, auditable results; LLMs handle ambiguity and strategy selection where they add real value
- **Operations research in AI pipelines** — LP solvers, greedy heuristics, and staged deployment aren't new techniques, but letting an LLM select between them based on situational awareness is a powerful pattern
- **Infrastructure as Code enables rapid iteration** — Terraform meant the entire stack could be rebuilt on a new AWS account in under an hour
- **Guardrails are a production necessity** — grounding checks on RAG output caught hallucinated medical thresholds that would have produced incorrect risk scores
